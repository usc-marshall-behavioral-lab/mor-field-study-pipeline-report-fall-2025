511 Triads Submitted. 32 Matched Sets Completed.

A pipeline analysis of the MOR Field Study survey response data, tracking 511 employee triads from initial contact submission through survey completion and attention-check screening. This report quantifies attrition at each stage and identifies factors associated with higher completion rates.

Alys Ferragamo, Brian Huh & Elena Lee

Companion report: Cross-Semester Analysis (Fall 2023 – Spring 2026)

511
Triads submitted
195
With ≥1 survey completed
32
Matched sets (all 3 roles completed)

Three factors account for most of the 94% pipeline loss (479 of 511 triads). First, a 65-day gap between contact submission and survey delivery means employees no longer recall agreeing to participate — and once surveys arrive, over half of those who open them leave within 5 minutes. Second, actual survey completion times (30–40 minutes median) exceed typical expectations for online surveys, and most respondents who open a link leave within the first 5 minutes. Third, contact characteristics are associated with completion rates: corporate emails are associated with approximately 3× the completion rate of personal emails, and triads sourced through family friends exhibit the highest completion rates among relationship types.

The most addressable near-term opportunity involves the 55 dyads where 2-of-3 surveys were started — in most cases, the missing respondent did not open their link. A targeted follow-up system for these near-misses, combined with reduced pipeline lag and shorter surveys, represents a direct path to increasing the number of usable matched sets.

02 — Who Responded

Respondent profile across all three roles

Demographic data for respondents who progressed far enough to reach the demographic questions. Sample sizes vary by measure and role.

Age pattern

Supervisors skew oldest (median 50), consistent with hierarchical seniority. Coworkers are youngest (median 38), and focal employees fall between the two (median 46). The 50–59 bracket is the largest group for both focal employees (34%, 43 of 126) and supervisors (38%, 34 of 89).

Diversity

The sample is racially diverse, with no single group exceeding 42% in any role. White and Asian respondents are roughly equally represented across all three roles. This reflects the demographics of USC Marshall's student network and the Los Angeles labor market.

Income skew

The sample skews high-income. Nearly half of supervisors (49%, 44 of 90) and 40% of focal employees (50 of 126) report household income above $200k. This is consistent with the sample being drawn from professional networks of business school students, but limits generalizability to lower-income or hourly-wage populations.

06 — Student Pipeline

Student pipeline: guided recruitment and contact submission

For Fall 2025, the entire student-facing experience was rebuilt — from revamped handouts and information packets to a contact submission form that doesn't just collect data, but actively teaches students the purpose and process of field research recruitment.

📦
Revamped handouts & info packets

Before students even opened the form, they received redesigned materials with concrete recruitment tools: talking points for approaching potential focal employees, outreach email templates, clear explanations of what participation involves, and guidance on navigating the ask.

🔍
Network mapping exercise

Before asking for any contacts, students were prompted to inventory their personal network — first by workplace type (corporate, retail, healthcare, government, trades) and then by relationship channel (family, friends' parents, community members, own workplace).

📋
Adaptive branching by readiness

Students self-assessed their current situation and were routed accordingly. Ready students moved straight to contact entry. Those still recruiting received targeted encouragement, including reminders that any full-time employee qualifies and a list of commonly overlooked sources.

🏢
Organization & relationship identification

Students named the specific organization where all three employees work and classified the industry type — making the triad concrete rather than abstract. They also formally identified their relationship to the focal employee.

🔔
Social proof & encouragement nudges

Students at various stages encountered motivational messaging and structured checkpoints. The encouragement block reminded students that "many students ask 3–5 people before finding all contacts" — normalizing the effort required.

✅
Pre-submission verification gates

Before finalizing, students confirmed four requirements: permission obtained from all three individuals, all employees at the same organization, email addresses verified, and understanding that surveys would arrive from mor.research@usc.edu.

Conversion performance

The guided approach achieved a 99% conversion rate for students who arrived ready and a 91% rate for those with partial contacts — indicating that the combination of upfront preparation materials and in-form scaffolding supported high conversion rates across readiness levels. Even among students who had not yet asked anyone, 57% completed a triad (17 of 30) after working through the guided flow.

[14]

Refinement opportunities

The redesigned form achieved strong conversion for ready students. These observations point to areas where the next iteration could recover more participants.

🚪
Exit ramps may be too final

Every non-ready path ended with an EndSurvey exit. Of ~88 non-ready students, 59 returned to complete the form. The remaining 29 did not. A "save progress and return" flow rather than a full exit could recover more.

🔒
33 lost at the permission gate

33 students made it to the pre-submission block, understood the requirements, then selected "No, I need to get permission first" — which ended the survey. These students had progressed to the final pre-submission stage and may respond to a follow-up reminder rather than a terminal exit.

📊
Social proof nudge needs recalibration

The encouragement messaging referenced a 95% success rate. The actual submission rate was closer to 76% (38 of 50 routed students). Aligning this figure with actual observed rates may improve the messaging's credibility.

🏢
"Same organization" remains the core bottleneck

The triad requirement means students must get their contact to recruit two colleagues. This dependency chain is a primary factor in lower supervisor and coworker response rates, as these individuals are one degree removed from the student's direct influence.

⏱
Survey length is a likely barrier to completion

Median completion times of 30–40 minutes represent a significant time commitment for respondents with no direct stake in the research. This length likely contributes to the high rate of immediate abandonment (1–5% progress drop-offs), particularly for respondents who assess the survey scope early and decide it exceeds their available time.

Impact on usable data

The focal survey has the highest fail rate (24.4%, 32 of 131) — which is also the most damaging since a focal failure breaks both the triad and any associated dyads. This is likely because the focal survey is the longest (~40 min median), leading to more fatigue-driven inattention.

Sample size implications

With 18–24 usable triads (18 strict, 24 soft), statistical power for detecting moderate effects (d = 0.5) in three-way analyses is limited. At α = .05 and n=18, power for a paired-samples t-test is approximately 0.34 — well below the conventional 0.80 threshold. The 55 usable dyads provide somewhat more power for paired analyses. Researchers should consider whether dyad-level analyses, relaxed attention check thresholds, or pooling across semesters may be necessary to achieve adequate power for planned statistical tests.

08 — Implemented

Changes deployed for Spring 2026

The following improvements, informed by findings from this pipeline analysis and cross-semester comparisons (§10), have been deployed for the Spring 2026 term.

📄
Updated and revamped program handouts

Redesigned student-facing handouts with clearer instructions, updated timelines, and streamlined language to reduce confusion about the research participation process and set accurate expectations. → View handout

🌐
Beta pilot website with additional templates

A dedicated website now provides students with ready-to-use templates that outline the full process step by step — what to say, what to expect, and how the triad works — giving them a centralized reference point beyond the in-class handout. → Visit site

💬
Talking points for texting and phone outreach

Pre-written talking points now available for students who contact their employees via text message or phone call rather than email. These scripts provide students with prepared language for real-time conversations, offering structure for conversations that might otherwise be difficult to navigate.

📧
Stronger language requiring company email domains

The contact collection form now includes explicit language directing students to provide company or organizational email addresses rather than personal ones. While no hard-halt validation is in place yet, the updated wording sets a clearer expectation at the point of entry. Early evidence from Spring 2026 submissions (§10): all-work-email triads increased from 36.6% to 56.4%, all-personal triads decreased from 30.8% to 19.9%, and within-triad domain matching rose from 58.6% to 79.3%. → See §10 Email Quality

⏳
+1 credit determination deferred until after Step 2B deadline

Students are no longer told whether they will need to complete the alternative +1 credit assignment until after the Step 2B contact submission deadline has passed. This removes the early exit incentive — previously, students who learned about the alternative path early had less motivation to invest time finding viable contacts. By deferring the determination, students are encouraged to use the full submission window to identify and secure employee participants rather than defaulting to the easier route.

❓
Employee-facing participant FAQ website

A dedicated FAQ site for workplace participants — separate from the student-facing pilot website — is now live. It addresses common employee questions: what the study is about, why they were contacted, how long the survey takes, how their data is used, and who to contact with questions. The site is designed to be linked in survey invitation emails and reminder messages, giving employees a direct resource rather than requiring them to contact the student who recruited them. → View FAQ site

🔄
Rolling survey distribution

Surveys will be launched on a rolling basis as valid contacts are submitted, rather than waiting for all submissions and distributing in large batches. The Fall 2025 batched approach compressed distribution into late November; cross-semester data indicates that semesters with earlier, faster distribution (Fall 2024: 4-day time-to-50%, Spring 2025: 3-day) exhibited higher conversion rates. → See §10 Cross-Semester Timing

🔍
Screening questions and readiness checks in Step 2B

The Step 2B form now routes students through readiness checks before contact submission. Students who have not yet identified all three contacts or secured permissions are directed to return later rather than submitting incomplete entries. This has reduced multi-submissions (37 vs 95 in Fall 2025) and increased the proportion of valid submissions.

Next: Eleven additional recommendations → §09 Recommendations

09 — Recommendations

Eleven recommendations for Spring 2026

Ordered by position in the pipeline: reduce lag, address survey length, recover near-misses, optimize contact selection, and improve data quality.

⚡
Pipeline & Delivery
5 recommendations — reduce lag, improve follow-up, accelerate cascades
Shorten the pipeline lag
HIGH
→ See §03 Timing

The 65-day median gap between contact submission and survey launch exceeds typical recall windows. Reducing this to 1–2 weeks, or sending an immediate confirmation email upon contact submission, may improve initial response rates.

Cross-semester evidence (§10): Fall 2024 (time-to-50%: 4 days) achieved an 80.5% FE conversion rate. Spring 2025 (3 days) achieved 37.5%. Fall 2025's batched approach (18-day compressed window) achieved 25.3%. Rolling distribution has been implemented for Spring 2026 to address this.
Front-load reminder frequency
HIGH
→ See §03 Timing

83% of completions (162 of 195) occurred within a 2-week window, with minimal responses after day 14. A compressed reminder sequence (e.g., Day 1, Day 4, Day 8) with escalating urgency may capture more respondents within this active window.

Build real-time near-miss follow-up
HIGH
→ See §05 Near-Misses

55 dyads had 2-of-3 surveys started, with the majority of missing respondents never having opened the survey. A targeted reminder system that identifies incomplete triads and contacts the missing respondent could increase both usable triad and dyad counts.

Send all 3 invitations simultaneously
MED
→ See §05 Near-Misses

In completed triads, the coworker or supervisor finished first 72% of the time (23 of 32 matched sets), with remaining members following within a median of 4.6 days. Simultaneous invitation delivery, combined with completion notifications, may accelerate cascading responses.

Auto-notify coworker and supervisor when the focal employee finishes
HIGH
→ See §05 Near-Misses

When the focal employee completes their survey, an automated email should be sent to the associated coworker and supervisor notifying them that the focal employee has finished and that it is now their turn. This creates social proof and a sense of reciprocity — the focal employee (their colleague) already invested the time, which reframes the request from an abstract ask to a concrete obligation. The email should include a direct link to their survey and a reply-to contact address for the research team so that recipients who encounter issues or have questions can resolve them immediately rather than abandoning the survey. Qualtrics supports completion-triggered email workflows through the "Send Email" action in survey flow, which can be configured to pull the coworker and supervisor email addresses from embedded data fields and fire automatically when the focal survey records a "Finished" status.

📋
Survey Experience
4 recommendations — reduce burden, support respondents, improve quality
Shorten the surveys
HIGH
→ See §03 Timing

Median completion times range from 30–40 minutes — a significant time commitment that likely contributes to early abandonment. Reducing survey length where possible, particularly for the focal survey (42-minute median, highest attention-check fail rate at 24.4% or 32 of 131), could improve completion rates and response quality.

Split each survey into two structured waves
HIGH
→ See §03 Timing

If surveys cannot be shortened, split each one into two defined waves (e.g., Wave 1: ~15 minutes, Wave 2: ~15 minutes) with a scheduled break in between rather than allowing arbitrary pause-and-resume. Two shorter sessions may reduce the perceived time commitment relative to one 30–40 minute block, which is associated with immediate abandonment at 1–5% progress. Wave 2 can be sent as a follow-up email 24–48 hours after Wave 1 completion, serving as both a continuation prompt and a progress indicator. Qualtrics supports this through survey flow branching with embedded data flags to track wave completion status.

Caveat: A two-wave approach introduces the risk of between-wave attrition — participants may complete Part 1 but fail to return for Part 2. However, cross-semester data (§10) indicates that the primary drop-off currently occurs before respondents begin the survey, not mid-survey. A shorter Part 1 may therefore increase the number of people who start, and retention strategies (timely Part 2 delivery, progress framing, completion incentives) can be designed to minimize between-wave loss. This tradeoff should be evaluated carefully before implementation.

Reduce attention-check attrition
HIGH
→ See §07 Quality

Attention-check filtering reduced 32 started triads to 18–24 usable — a 25–44% loss at the final pipeline stage depending on threshold (8 to 14 of 32 matched sets lost). The focal survey (5 checks, 24.4% fail rate) accounts for the largest share. Options to reduce this attrition include fewer checks per survey, repositioning checks earlier when engagement is higher, or applying a partial-pass threshold (e.g., 4 of 5) rather than requiring a perfect score. Each triad lost at this stage represents a fully completed three-person data collection effort.

🎓
Student Coaching & Data Entry
3 recommendations — contact selection guidance, input validation
Coach students toward "family friends"
MED
→ See §04 Contacts

Family friends showed the highest triad completion rate (9.6%, 5 of 52), above immediate family (7.5%, 3 of 40) and personal friends (2.2%, 4 of 185). Guiding students toward family friends in professional settings as their primary Employee 1 target may improve downstream completion.

Spring 2026 context (§10): Approximately 63% of Spring 2026 focal employees are sourced through family connections (43% immediate family, 6% extended, 14% family friends). This is a common path for undergraduates. The recommendation is to optimize within family contacts by steering students toward family friends in professional settings over immediate family members — not to increase family-based recruitment overall.
Warn about low-yield industries
LOW
→ See §04 Contacts

Retail (0 of 24), construction (0 of 8), and non-profit (0 of 16) organizations produced zero complete matched sets in Fall 2025. Surfacing this information during the network assessment stage and suggesting alternative sectors may help students select higher-yield contacts. Spring 2026 submissions show a similar sector distribution (§10), with small/medium businesses (27.3%) and large corporations (15.0%) leading.

Embed hard-halt validation in the email entry form
HIGH
→ See §04 Contacts · See §07 Quality · See §08 Implemented

The contact form already includes stronger language directing students toward work emails (see §08), which is associated with a shift from 36.6% to 56.4% all-work triads in Spring 2026. The next step is implementing real-time, in-form validation that prevents submission when the same email appears in more than one role field (focal, supervisor, coworker), when a @usc.edu address is entered in any employee field, or when the domain matches a predetermined list of personal email providers (e.g., gmail.com, yahoo.com, hotmail.com, outlook.com, icloud.com, aol.com). Given that corporate emails are associated with approximately 3× the triad completion rate, blocking personal domains at entry — with an inline message explaining that a work email is required and why — may reduce the 19.9% of triads submitted with all-personal emails. Qualtrics supports custom JavaScript validation and embedded data checks that can enforce these rules without requiring a separate screening step.

Path forward

Summary. Eight changes have been implemented for Spring 2026: stronger email language with screening questions, deferred +1 credit determination, revised handouts, a student-facing pilot website, outreach talking points, an employee-facing FAQ site, rolling survey distribution, and readiness checks in the Step 2B form. The eleven recommendations above address remaining pipeline bottlenecks: survey duration, follow-up cadence, near-miss recovery, and attention-check attrition.

Early evidence from Spring 2026 submissions (§10) indicates that the screening and email requirements are associated with measurable changes in contact quality: all-work-email triads rose from 36.6% to 56.4%, within-triad domain matching increased from 58.6% to 79.3%, and multi-submissions decreased from 95 to 37 students. These are pre-distribution metrics; downstream conversion rates will be available after survey distribution is complete.

Cross-semester analysis (§10) also identifies Fall 2024 as a reference point — the semester with the fewest submitted triads (195) but the highest conversion rate (80.5% FE), associated with early distribution and a 4-day time-to-50%. The combination of higher-quality submissions and rolling distribution in Spring 2026 aims to replicate those conditions.

Watch items for Spring 2026

Patterns in the Spring 2026 data worth monitoring as we move into distribution.

.edu emails — 15% of submissions

83 contact emails use usc.edu domains and another 10 use marshall.usc.edu. Combined with the jump in higher education orgs (6.8% → 13.1%), this could indicate legitimate USC staff — or students submitting campus contacts who don't fit the intended participant profile. Worth cross-referencing against faculty/staff directories before distribution.

Family connections — 63% of focal employees

Roughly 43% of focal employees are immediate family members (parent, sibling, spouse). Adding extended family (6%) and family friends (14%) brings the total to about 63% coming through family channels. This is a natural path for undergraduates, but worth being aware of from a data independence standpoint.

Remaining personal emails

Despite the new screening language, about 20% of triads still use all-personal emails. The top personal domains remain gmail.com (dominant), yahoo.com, and a handful of qq.com addresses. Based on prior semesters, these contacts may convert at lower rates. However, the overall email type distribution has shifted toward work emails relative to all prior semesters.
